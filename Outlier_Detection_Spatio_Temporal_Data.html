Outlier

What is Outlier?

An outlier is an observation that differs so much from other observations as to arouse suspicion that it was generated by a different mechanism [1].

Outlier in 1-Dimension

In one dimension, an observation is considered an outlier if it is unusually large or unusually small compared to the remaining observations.

Methods to Detect Outliers

  • Z-score Method
  • IQR (Interquartile Range) Method
  • Boxplot Method
  • Median Absolute Deviation (MAD)

Defining Outliers

Different Object

A dog among cats can be considered an outlier because it differs substantially from the majority group.

Extreme Height

An extremely tall individual compared to the surrounding population may represent an outlier.

Distinct Observation

An observation that differs strongly from the majority pattern can be flagged as an outlier.

Definitions

1. Amplitude Outliers

An amplitude outlier is a functional observation that follows a similar overall shape as the majority of curves but has unusually large or small magnitude variations.

\[ y(t) = a\,\mu(t) \]

2. Magnitude Outliers

A magnitude outlier is a functional observation whose values are consistently larger or smaller than the central function while preserving a similar shape.

\[ y(t) = \mu(t) + c \]

3. Shape Outliers

A shape outlier is a functional observation whose overall pattern or shape differs from the central tendency of the sample, regardless of its magnitude or amplitude.

\[ y(t) \not\approx a \cdot \mu(t) + c \]

4. Trend Outliers

Trend outliers are curves that exhibit unusual temporal patterns compared to the majority of observations in the dataset.

Type Example
Linear trend \(a + bt\)
Polynomial trend \(a + bt + ct^{2}\)
Exponential trend \(ae^{bt}\)
Cyclic trend Repeated periodic fluctuations over time

5. Depth Outliers

A data depth concept can be viewed as a generalization of quantiles to multivariate data.
It measures the centrality (depth) of a given point in \(\mathbb{R}^n\), assuming
\(X_1, \ldots, X_k\) is a random sample from \(\mathbb{R}^n\) [5], [6]


Mahalanobis Depth

\[ D_M(x)=\frac{1}{1+(x-\mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)} \] [5]

Here, \(\mu\) and \(\Sigma\) denote the mean vector and covariance matrix, respectively. Larger depth values indicate more central observations.

6. Frequency Outlier

Frequency outliers are curves whose oscillation frequency differs significantly from that of the majority of curves in the dataset.

\[ y(t)=\sin(2\pi f t) \]

where (f) denotes the oscillation frequency.

\[ f=\frac{1}{T} \]

where (T) is the period of oscillation.

7. Distance Outlier

Distance outliers are functional observations whose distance from a central reference curve, such as the mean or median curve, is unusually large or unusually small compared to the majority of curves in the dataset.

\[ d_i = d\!\left(y_i(t),\, \mu(t)\right) \]

where \(mu(t)\) denotes the central curve and \(d_i\)is the distance measure.

\[ d_i > Q_{1-\alpha} \]

may be classified as a distance outlier, where \(Q_{1-\alpha}\) denotes the \((1-\alpha)\)-quantile of the distance distribution.

1. Spatial Variation in Temporal Trends (SVTT)

  • Detects regions with unusual temporal trends.

  • Compares trends inside and outside a spatial window.

  • Implemented in SaTScan using the Discrete Poisson Model.

  • Types:

    • Linear SVTT
    • Quadratic SVTT

\[ H_0:\text{Trend}_{in}=\text{Trend}_{out} \]

\[ H_1:\text{Trend}_{in}\neq \text{Trend}_{out} \]

2. Trend-Based Outliers

Let

\[ y_1(t), y_2(t), \ldots, y_k(t) \]

denote the temporal functions of \(k\) regions observed at time points

\[ t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n. \]

Algorithm

Step 1:
Fit a suitable trend model separately for each region using a small number of parameters, such as Linear, Quadratic, and Exponential trends.

For example, a linear trend model may be written as

\[ y_i(t)=\beta_{0i}+\beta_{1i}t+\varepsilon_i(t), \]

where \(\beta_{0i}\) and \(\beta_{1i}\) represent the trend parameters for region \(i\).

Step 2:
Treat the estimated parameter vectors

\[ (\hat{\beta}_{0i}, \hat{\beta}_{1i}) \]

as observations in lower-dimensional space and apply standard outlier detection techniques.

Step 3:
Regions whose parameter vectors are detected as unusual are identified as trend-based outliers.

3. Distance-Based Outliers

Let

\[ y_1(t), y_2(t), \ldots, y_k(t) \]

denote the temporal functions of \(k\) regions observed at time points

\[ t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n. \]

Algorithm

Step 1:
Compute the distance between each curve and a central curve, such as the mean or median curve.

For example, the Euclidean distance between curve \(y_i(t)\) and the central curve \(c(t)\) is

\[ d_i= \sqrt{ \sum_{j=1}^{n} \left( y_i(t_j)-c(t_j) \right)^2 }. \]

Step 2:
Use the obtained distances as one-dimensional observations and apply standard outlier detection methods.

Step 3:
Curves having unusually large distances from the central curve are identified as distance-based outliers.

3. Commonly Used Distance Measures

Euclidean Distance

\[ d(x,y)= \sqrt{ \sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-y_i)^2 } \]

Manhattan Distance

\[ d(x,y)= \sum_{i=1}^{n}|x_i-y_i| \]

Mahalanobis Distance

\[ d(x,y)= \sqrt{ (x-y)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(x-y) } \]

Cosine Distance

\[ d(x,y)= 1- \frac{x^\top y} {\|x\|\|y\|} \]

4. Depth-Based Outliers

Let

\[ y_1(t), y_2(t), \ldots, y_k(t) \]

denote the temporal functions of \(k\) regions observed at time points

\[ t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n. \]

Algorithm

Step 1:
Compute a depth value for each curve, which measures the centrality of the curve relative to the remaining curves.

A general depth function may be written as

\[ D(y_i \mid Y), \]

where \(Y=\{y_1,y_2,\ldots,y_k\}\) denotes the collection of all curves.

Step 2:
Use the obtained depth values as one-dimensional observations and apply standard outlier detection methods.

Step 3:
Curves with unusually small depth values are identified as depth-based outliers.

Commonly Used Depth Measures

Mahalanobis Depth

\[ D_M(x)= \frac{ 1 }{ 1+(x-\mu)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu) } \]

Spatial Depth

\[ D_S(x)= 1- \left\| \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{x-X_i}{\|x-X_i\|} \right\| \]

Tukey Depth

\[ D_T(x)= \inf_{u\in S^{d-1}} P(u^\top X \le u^\top x) \]

Modified Band Depth (MBD)

\[ \text{MBD}(x)= \binom{n}{2}^{-1} \sum_{i<j} \frac{ \lambda(A(x;x_i,x_j)) }{ \lambda(T) } \]

  • \(A(x;x_i,x_j)\) : set where \(x(t)\) lies inside the band formed by \(x_i(t)\) and \(x_j(t)\)

  • \(\lambda(\cdot)\) : Lebesgue measure

  • \(T\) : observation time domain

  • \(X\) : random vector

  • \(u\) : projection direction

  • \(S^{d-1}\) : unit sphere in \(\mathbb{R}^d\)

Metrics Used for Analysis

Sensitivity

\[ \text{Sensitivity}= \frac{TP}{TP+FN} \]

Proportion of true outliers correctly identified.

Specificity

\[ \text{Specificity}= \frac{TN}{TN+FP} \]

Proportion of normal observations correctly identified.

Positive Predictive Value (PPV)

\[ \text{PPV}= \frac{TP}{TP+FP} \]

Proportion of detected outliers that are truly outliers.

Purity

\[ \text{Purity}= \frac{1}{N}\sum_k \max_j |c_k \cap t_j| \]

Measures homogeneity within detected groups.

Rand Index (RI)

\[ RI= \frac{TP+TN}{TP+TN+FP+FN} \]

Measures similarity between true and detected classifications.

Adjusted Rand Index (ARI)

\[ ARI= \frac{RI-\mathbb{E}(RI)} {\max(RI)-\mathbb{E}(RI)} \]

Chance-adjusted version of RI.

Jaccard Index

\[ J= \frac{TP}{TP+FP+FN} \]

Measures overlap between detected and true outlier sets.

Computation Time

Average time required to perform outlier detection for 1 dataset.
